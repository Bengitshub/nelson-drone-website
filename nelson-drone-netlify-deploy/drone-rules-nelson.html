Can you fly a drone in Nelson? Your guide to the local drone rules

Short answer: yes, you can fly a drone in Nelson — but it's one of the trickier corners of New Zealand to do it legally. The city, Richmond and much of the Waimea plains sit beneath Nelson Airport's controlled airspace, and the region's best scenery, like Abel Tasman, is protected conservation land. Get it wrong and you're not just breaking a rule — you could be putting crewed aircraft at risk.

This guide sets out the drone rules in Nelson in plain English: the national CAA Part 101 basics, the airport airspace question, the 4km aerodrome rule, shielded flying, and where you need a permit. It's written for anyone flying here — and it's the same groundwork a compliant commercial operator sorts before a shoot.

The drone rules in Nelson start with CAA Part 101

Every drone flight in New Zealand under 25kg falls under the Civil Aviation Authority's Part 101 rules. You don't need a pilot licence to fly under Part 101, but you do have to stay inside a clear set of limits. These apply the same whether you're a hobbyist filming the back yard or a business shooting a promo. Get across them before anything else.

  • Fly no higher than 120m (400ft) above the ground.
  • Fly only in daylight, and keep the drone within your line of sight at all times.
  • Give way to all crewed aircraft — they always have right of way.
  • Only fly one drone at a time, and keep it under 25kg.
  • Don't fly over people or private property without their consent.
  • Know the airspace you're in before you take off.

Flying FPV? You'll need a spotter

First-person-view (FPV) drones flown through goggles are a special case. Because the pilot is watching a live video feed rather than the aircraft itself, Part 101's 'visual line of sight' requirement isn't met by the pilot alone. The fix is simple and required: a second person — a spotter, or observer — keeps eyes on the drone the whole time and stays in constant communication with the pilot. That's why professional FPV shoots always run as a two-person crew.

The big one in Nelson: airport controlled airspace

This is what catches most people out. Nelson Airport sits right in town, and the controlled airspace around it covers a large slice of Nelson, Richmond and the Waimea plains. To fly a drone inside controlled airspace you need authorisation from air traffic control — you can't just launch. In New Zealand that's requested through AirShare, the national drone hub, which passes your flight details to Airways for approval.

  • Check the AirShare map before you fly — it shows the controlled-airspace boundaries around Nelson Airport.
  • Authorisation takes time to come through, so it's not a spur-of-the-moment thing.
  • Flying in controlled airspace without clearance puts crewed aircraft at risk — and breaks the law.

The 4km aerodrome rule

Separate from controlled airspace, Part 101 says you must not fly within 4km of any aerodrome unless certain conditions are met. That covers Nelson Airport, but also smaller airstrips and helipads you might not think about. Inside that 4km zone you generally need an agreement with the aerodrome operator or clearance from air traffic control — unless you're flying a shielded operation, covered next.

Shielded operations: the low-level exception

There's an important exception built into the rules. A 'shielded operation' means flying within 100m of, and below the top of, a natural or man-made object — a building, a stand of trees, a hillside. Because that object shields your drone from crewed aircraft, shielded flying can let you operate close to an aerodrome, or inside controlled airspace, without ATC clearance. It's a genuinely useful tool for low-level filming in town, but it has to be done properly, staying tucked in close to the shielding object for the whole flight.

Flying over people and private property

Part 101 requires the consent of anyone you fly over, and of the owner or occupier of the property you're flying above or taking off from. In practice that means squaring things away with neighbours, getting the landowner's OK, and keeping well clear of crowds. Local councils count too: Nelson City and Tasman District both ask for permission before you take off from or land in their parks and reserves, including busy spots like Tahunanui.

Abel Tasman and other DOC conservation land

Some of the region's most photogenic places — Abel Tasman National Park, Kahurangi, the coastal reserves — are public conservation land managed by the Department of Conservation. You need DOC's authorisation to fly a drone over, or take off and land on, that land, and commercial work needs a concession. DOC manages this to protect wildlife and other visitors' experience, so approval isn't a given. If a shoot involves a national park, plan well ahead.

The straightforward way: let a compliant operator handle it

None of this makes Nelson a no-fly zone — it just means the paperwork matters. Most commercial work is done entirely within Part 101; going beyond those standard limits is where CAA approval under Part 102 comes in. FAQ

Questions & answers

Do I need a licence to fly a drone in Nelson?

No. Flying under CAA Part 101 doesn't require a pilot licence or certificate, even for commercial video — you just have to stay within the Part 101 rules. Going beyond those standard limits is where CAA approval under Part 102 comes in.

Can you fly a drone at Tahunanui Beach or in central Nelson?

Both sit under Nelson Airport's controlled airspace and within 4km of the aerodrome, so you can't just turn up and fly. You'd need air traffic control authorisation through AirShare, or a low-level shielded operation — plus council permission to take off from the reserve.

Can I fly a drone in Abel Tasman National Park?

Only with authorisation from the Department of Conservation, which manages the park. Commercial filming needs a concession, and approval isn't guaranteed, so plan well ahead.

Do I need permission to fly near Nelson Airport?

Yes. Within controlled airspace or 4km of the airport you need ATC clearance (requested via AirShare) or an agreement with the aerodrome — unless you're flying a shielded operation, staying low and close to a building or trees.
